Selling a business is rarely as simple as agreeing on a price and handing over the keys. While the “headline price”—the dollar amount written on the Letter of Intent (LOI)—gets all the attention, seasoned dealmakers know that the real value of a transaction is determined in the definitive purchase agreement.

This 50-to-100-page legal document is where your “exit” actually happens. It is also where value can be eroded, risks can be shifted, and the “cash at close” can be drastically reduced.

In Mergers and Acquisitions (M&A), the devil is truly in the details. A high valuation means nothing if the terms and conditions allow the buyer to claw back money, lock up your funds for years, or restrict your future livelihood. Whether you are on the buy-side or the sell-side, here are the eight critical terms you must scrutinize before signing on the dotted line.

1. Indemnification: The “Clawback” Mechanism
--------------------------------------------

If there is one section that keeps M&A lawyers awake at night, it is indemnification. This clause outlines the circumstances under which the seller must reimburse the buyer for post-closing losses.

Essentially, if the buyer discovers a liability after the deal closes (e.g., an undisclosed lawsuit, a tax error, or a breach of contract), they can come after the seller for damages.

**What to watch out for:**

* **Survival Period:** How long are you on the hook? Buyers want this to be indefinite; sellers want it capped at 12–18 months.
* **The “Basket”:** This acts like an insurance deductible. It establishes a threshold of losses (e.g., $50,000) that must be reached before the buyer can claim indemnification. Ensure this is a “deductible basket” (you pay only the amount *over* the threshold) rather than a “tipping basket” (once the threshold is hit, you pay the *entire* amount back to dollar one).

2. Earn-Outs: The “Maybe” Money
-------------------------------

An earn-out is a mechanism where a portion of the purchase price is contingent on the business achieving future performance goals, such as revenue or EBITDA targets.

Buyers love earn-outs because they reduce upfront risk. Sellers generally dislike them because they lose control of the company post-closing, making it harder to hit those targets.

**The danger:**  
If you agree to an earn-out, you must negotiate clear covenants regarding how the business will be run. If the buyer integrates your company and cuts your marketing budget or fires your sales team, you might miss your targets and lose millions. The terms must specify that the buyer cannot intentionally impede your ability to hit the earn-out goals.

3. Working Capital Adjustments
------------------------------

This is the most common place where purchase prices get “chipped away” at the last minute.

The deal assumes the business comes with a “normal” amount of working capital (current assets minus current liabilities) to operate on day one. If the actual working capital at closing is lower than the target set in the agreement, the seller owes the buyer the difference dollar-for-dollar.

**The trap:**  
Buyers often try to set the “target” working capital artificially high. If they succeed, you will almost certainly fall short at closing and have to write them a check, effectively lowering your sale price. Scrutinize the historical average calculation used to set this target.

4. Representations and Warranties (Reps & Warranties)
-----------------------------------------------------

“Reps and Warranties” are the facts the seller claims are true about the business (e.g., “We have no pending litigation,” “Our IP is valid,” “We have paid all taxes”).

If a representation turns out to be false, it triggers the indemnification clause mentioned above.

**The nuance:**  
Watch out for “knowledge qualifiers.” As a seller, you want to limit your reps to “actual knowledge.”

* **Bad:** “The company has never infringed on any IP.” (If you accidentally did 5 years ago without knowing, you are liable).
* **Good:** “To the best of the Seller's Knowledge, the company has not infringed on any IP.”

5. The “Sandbagging” Clause
---------------------------

Sandbagging sounds exactly like what it is: a dirty trick. A pro-sandbagging clause allows the buyer to sue you for a breach of warranty *even if they knew about the breach before the deal closed.*

**Example:** During due diligence, the buyer notices a minor error in your customer contracts. They say nothing. They sign the deal. Then, a week later, they sue you for indemnification regarding those contracts to get money back.  
Always fight for an **anti-sandbagging provision**, which states that the buyer cannot claim damages for things they were aware of prior to closing.

6. Escrow and Holdbacks
-----------------------

It is standard practice for a buyer to hold back a percentage of the purchase price (usually 10-20%) in an escrow account to cover potential indemnification claims.

**What to negotiate:**

* **Amount:** Fight to keep this as low as possible (e.g., 5-10%).
* **Duration:** The escrow period should match the survival period of your reps and warranties (typically 12-18 months). Do not allow a buyer to hold 20% of your cash for three years “just in case.”

7. Non-Compete and Non-Solicit Agreements
-----------------------------------------

When you sell your business, the buyer wants to ensure you don't turn around the next day, start a rival company, and steal all your old clients. This is fair. However, overly aggressive non-competes can destroy your career.

**Red flags:**

* **Geographic Scope:** “Worldwide” restrictions are often unenforceable but can still be a headache. Ensure the geography is limited to where you actually did business.
* **Duration:** A 3-to-5-year non-compete is standard. Anything longer is excessive.
* **Scope of Activity:** Make sure the definition of “competitive business” isn't so broad that it prevents you from working in tangentially related industries or consulting.

8. Material Adverse Change (MAC) Clause
---------------------------------------

The MAC clause is the buyer's escape hatch. It allows them to walk away from the deal between signing and closing if the business suffers a “material adverse change.”

**The negotiation:**  
This definition must be tight. A drop in quarterly earnings should not trigger a MAC. It should be reserved for catastrophic events. furthermore, ensure there are “carve-outs” for general economic downturns or industry-wide issues. If the whole stock market crashes, the buyer shouldn't be able to single you out and cancel the deal.

Conclusion: Value is Protected in the Fine Print
------------------------------------------------

In the adrenaline rush of closing a deal, it is tempting to gloss over these terms to “just get it done.” This is a mistake. A higher purchase price with terrible terms is often worth less than a slightly lower price with clean, favorable terms.

The goal of an M&A negotiation is not just to sell the business, but to ensure that the money you receive is actually yours to keep. Work closely with experienced M&A counsel to navigate these clauses, because in the world of high-stakes transactions, what you don't know *will* cost you.
