In high-stakes industries such as nuclear power, manufacturing, and chemical processing, **accident prevention and risk management** are critical. The **Management Oversight and Risk Tree (MORT)** provides a structured, analytical method for investigating accidents and identifying their root causes. When paired with **Six Sigma**, MORT becomes a powerful tool for **enhancing operational safety, improving process reliability, and reducing costly incidents**.

MORT focuses on understanding **why losses occur**, whether due to preventable failures or risks that were assumed but carefully assessed. This article explores the **general approach to MORT analysis**, including its methodology, key steps, and practical applications.

---

### Understanding Accidents and Losses in MORT

In MORT terminology, an **accident is defined as an unplanned event that produces harm or damage**, also referred to as losses. Losses happen when a **harmful agent contacts a vulnerable target**, either because:

1. **Preventive measures fail**, or
2. It is an **assumed risk** that has been properly evaluated and accepted.

A core principle of MORT is that **analysis always begins by examining failures**, rather than assumed risks. By focusing on failures first, analysts can uncover gaps in **control systems, protective barriers, planning, design, and policy**.

---

### The Role of Barrier Analysis

Before conducting MORT analysis, it is essential to **identify key episodes** in the sequence of events leading to the incident. Each episode can be characterized as:

* A **vulnerable target** exposed to
* An **agent of harm** in the
* **Absence of adequate barriers**

To identify these episodes, MORT relies on a technique called **Barrier Analysis**, or more formally, **Energy Trace and Barrier Analysis (ETBA)**. Barrier analysis is critical because it **focuses the MORT investigation** on areas with the highest potential for uncovering causal factors.

Without barrier analysis, applying MORT effectively is challenging—even superficially. By tracing energy flows, hazards, and the protective barriers that failed or succeeded, analysts can pinpoint **where the system or process broke down**.

---

### The MORT Process: A Structured Dialogue

MORT analysis is best described as a **dialogue between the MORT questions and the situation under investigation**. The analyst acts as an **interpreter**, translating the generic MORT questions into insights about the real-world incident.

Key aspects of this process include:

1. **Sequential Questioning**
   * MORT provides a **predefined sequence of questions** designed to clarify facts surrounding an incident.
   * Analysts assess the relevance of each question, answering those pertinent to the situation.
2. **Note-Taking and Color Coding**
   * Analysts make **brief notes directly on the MORT chart**, summarizing findings and observations.
   * A **color-coding system** is commonly used:
     + **Red:** Problem identified
     + **Green:** Issue satisfactory
     + **Blue:** Relevant but insufficient information
   * Irrelevant questions are **crossed out**, showing that they were considered and excluded.
3. **Focused Analysis**
   * MORT is applied selectively, focusing on episodes where it **adds value to the investigation**.
   * One sweep through the MORT chart for a single episode can take about **one hour** for an experienced analyst.

---

### Outcomes of a MORT Analysis

When applied correctly, MORT provides several tangible benefits:

1. **Creation of New Lines of Enquiry**
   * MORT often reveals **previously overlooked causal factors**, guiding investigators to explore new areas.
2. **Visibility of Causal Factors**
   * Causes are **grouped thematically**, making it easier to understand systemic issues in processes, management, and human factors.
3. **Increased Confidence in Investigation Thoroughness**
   * By systematically addressing each MORT question, analysts can **demonstrate the rigor and completeness** of their investigation.

These outcomes are the result of a **structured, disciplined approach**, rather than intuitive guesswork. MORT ensures that **all critical angles are considered**, minimizing the likelihood of missing key factors.

---

### Best Practices for Using MORT in Six Sigma Initiatives

1. **Know When to Use It**
   * Only apply MORT when it **adds value to an investigation**. Using it without justification can waste time and resources.
2. **Familiarize Yourself with the Method**
   * Analysts should **practice on real incidents** to understand the nuances of the MORT questions and logic flow.
3. **Integrate Barrier Analysis**
   * Use **Energy Trace and Barrier Analysis** to identify high-priority episodes for MORT investigation.
4. **Document Thoroughly**
   * Take detailed notes and maintain **color-coded charts** to ensure transparency and facilitate review.
5. **Combine with Six Sigma Tools**
   * MORT complements Six Sigma methodologies like **DMAIC** by **identifying process failures and their root causes**, enabling targeted improvements.

---

### Conclusion

The **Management Oversight and Risk Tree (MORT)** is more than just a logic diagram; it is a **systematic framework for understanding failures, human factors, and management oversights** that lead to incidents. When combined with **Six Sigma principles**, MORT empowers organizations to **improve safety, strengthen processes, and minimize operational risk**.

By following a structured approach—beginning with **barrier analysis, sequential questioning, and thorough documentation**—organizations can achieve **deeper insights into incidents**, develop new lines of enquiry, and increase confidence in their investigations. MORT is not just a tool; it is a **philosophy of meticulous, evidence-based risk management** that has proven its value across industries for decades.
