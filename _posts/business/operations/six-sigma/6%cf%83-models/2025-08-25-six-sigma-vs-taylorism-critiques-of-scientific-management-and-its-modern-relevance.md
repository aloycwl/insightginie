In the early 20th century, **Frederick Winslow Taylor** introduced *Scientific Management*, also known as **Taylorism**, a framework that sought to optimize work by discovering the “one best way” to perform a task. While revolutionary at the time, Taylorism has been subject to extensive criticism for its rigidity, lack of human consideration, and overemphasis on efficiency at the expense of creativity.

In contrast, modern methodologies like **Six Sigma**, **Management by Objectives (MBO)**, **Continuous Improvement**, and **Business Process Reengineering (BPR)** embrace flexibility, teamwork, and data-driven strategies. This article explores the **critiques of Taylorism** and examines how approaches like Six Sigma have evolved to address its shortcomings.
