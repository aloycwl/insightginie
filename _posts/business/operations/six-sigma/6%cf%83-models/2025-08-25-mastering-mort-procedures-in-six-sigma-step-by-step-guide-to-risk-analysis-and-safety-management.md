<p>The <strong>Management Oversight and Risk Tree (MORT)</strong> is a cornerstone of advanced risk management in industries where safety, reliability, and operational excellence are critical. When integrated with <strong>Six Sigma methodologies</strong>, MORT provides a systematic framework for identifying failures, evaluating barriers, and implementing corrective measures.</p>

<p>Understanding <strong>MORT procedures</strong> ensures that organizations can conduct thorough investigations, mitigate risks proactively, and optimize process performance. This article provides a detailed walkthrough of <strong>MORT procedural steps</strong>, highlighting practical applications and best practices.</p>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Step 1: Selecting an Episode from Barrier Analysis</h3>

<p>MORT analysis begins with choosing a specific episode identified in a <strong>Barrier Analysis</strong>—a process that maps energy flows, hazards, and protective barriers in operations.</p>

<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Write the chosen episode on the MORT chart above <strong>SA1 (“Incident”)</strong>.</li>

<li>Begin at <strong>SB1 (“Harmful energy flow…”)</strong>, clearly stating the energy flow and its potential to cause harm.</li>

<li>Progress through the chart <strong>top to bottom, left to right</strong>, maintaining a systematic approach.</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Color-Coding Standards:</strong></p>

<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>RED:</strong> Problem confirmed with supporting evidence.</li>

<li><strong>GREEN:</strong> Control measures or processes meet established standards.</li>

<li><strong>BLUE:</strong> Evidence insufficient or standard uncertain; further investigation required.</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Best Practices:</strong></p>

<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Maintain a running <strong>list of further enquiries</strong> to ensure no potential failure or control gap is overlooked.</li>

<li>Document any <strong>provisional Assumed Risks</strong> during analysis for follow-up review.</li>
</ul>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Step 2: Completing SB3 – Controls &amp; Barriers LTA</h3>

<p>After evaluating <strong>SB3 (“Controls &amp; Barriers Less Than Adequate”)</strong>, the next focus is the <strong>M-branch</strong>, which examines management and systemic control factors.</p>

<p>Two approaches can be used:</p>

<ol class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Ad hoc exploration</strong> of the M-branch, following emerging insights from the SB1-SB3 analysis.</li>

<li><strong>Sequential exploration</strong> using the defined nodes: <strong>a2-MB1, a1-MB1, MA1, MA2, MB2</strong>.</li>
</ol>

<p>This step ensures that <strong>organizational and management factors contributing to the incident</strong> are systematically evaluated alongside operational controls.</p>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Step 3: Analyzing Additional Episodes</h3>

<p>If required, select another episode from the <strong>Barrier Analysis</strong> and repeat the SA1 steps using a <strong>fresh MORT chart</strong>.</p>

<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>This iterative approach allows multiple risk scenarios to be examined independently.</li>

<li>Ensures that <strong>all critical episodes in the process</strong> are evaluated for potential oversights and deficiencies.</li>
</ul>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Step 4: Moving to SA2 – Amelioration</h3>

<p>Once <strong>all SA1 analyses</strong> are complete:</p>

<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Mark which episodes from the Barrier Analysis have undergone MORT analysis.</li>

<li>Move to <strong>SA2 (“Amelioration”)</strong>, focusing on corrective and mitigating measures after incidents.</li>

<li>Explore the <strong>M-branch</strong> in light of SA2 findings, assessing whether management controls adequately support risk mitigation.</li>
</ul>

<p>This phase shifts attention from <strong>incident causation</strong> to <strong>post-event management and process improvement</strong>.</p>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Step 5: Reviewing Provisional Assumed Risks</h3>

<p>Provisional Assumed Risks noted in previous steps must be carefully reviewed:</p>

<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Assess if they are <strong>Less Than Adequate (LTA)</strong> using nodes such as <strong>a1-MB1</strong>.</li>

<li>Evaluate whether these assumed risks require additional controls, procedural changes, or further investigation.</li>
</ul>

<p>This step ensures that <strong>all known and potential risks are addressed</strong> systematically before finalizing recommendations.</p>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Step 6: Reviewing MB2</h3>

<p>Next, review <strong>MB2 (“Management Branch 2”)</strong> in light of the analysis conducted so far:</p>

<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Confirm that <strong>management and oversight functions</strong> are properly evaluated.</li>

<li>Identify gaps in policies, supervision, training, or resources that could compromise safety or operational efficiency.</li>
</ul>

<p>This step integrates <strong>SA1, SA2, and M-branch insights</strong> into a coherent assessment of both operational and managerial factors.</p>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Step 7: Overview of M-Branch Issues</h3>

<p>The final procedural step is a comprehensive review of all <strong>M-branch issues</strong>:</p>

<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Consider the <strong>overall systemic risk picture</strong>, integrating findings from specific and amelioration branches.</li>

<li>Document the results of the analysis, including <strong>causal factors, risk controls, and recommended improvements</strong>.</li>

<li>Ensure that lessons learned feed into <strong>future Barrier Analyses and Six Sigma process improvements</strong>.</li>
</ul>

<p>This holistic review solidifies the value of MORT as a <strong>structured tool for risk management</strong> and ensures that all contributing factors are captured.</p>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Practical Tips for MORT Procedure Implementation</h3>

<ol class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Use Evidence-Based Coding:</strong> Only assign <strong>RED, GREEN, or BLUE</strong> based on verifiable data. Avoid assumptions that could compromise the reliability of the analysis.</li>

<li><strong>Document Assumed Risks:</strong> Keep a running table of <strong>provisional and confirmed assumed risks</strong> to ensure no detail is overlooked.</li>

<li><strong>Iterate When Needed:</strong> Complex operations may require multiple MORT charts and repeated analyses of episodes to cover all risks comprehensively.</li>

<li><strong>Integrate with Six Sigma:</strong> Apply findings to <strong>DMAIC or DFSS projects</strong>, ensuring that risk insights lead to measurable process improvements.</li>

<li><strong>Engage Trained Analysts:</strong> MORT procedures are <strong>professional tools</strong>, requiring experience and judgment to apply correctly.</li>
</ol>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Conclusion</h3>

<p>MORT procedures provide a <strong>step-by-step, systematic method</strong> for identifying operational failures, management oversights, and assumed risks. By combining <strong>Barrier Analysis, SA1 and SA2 evaluations, and thorough M-branch exploration</strong>, organizations can ensure comprehensive <strong>risk identification, mitigation, and continuous improvement</strong>.</p>

<p>When integrated with <strong>Six Sigma initiatives</strong>, MORT not only enhances <strong>process safety and reliability</strong> but also supports <strong>data-driven decision-making, root cause analysis, and operational excellence</strong>.</p>

<p>Mastering MORT procedures equips organizations with a <strong>powerful toolset to proactively manage risks, prevent losses, and sustain high standards of safety and performance</strong>.</p>
