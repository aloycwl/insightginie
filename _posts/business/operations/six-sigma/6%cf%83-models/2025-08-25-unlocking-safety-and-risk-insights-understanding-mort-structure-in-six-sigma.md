<p>In complex industries, accidents and losses can carry enormous financial, operational, and reputational costs. The <strong>Management Oversight and Risk Tree (MORT)</strong> provides a structured, analytical approach for <strong>identifying the root causes of losses and improving safety and operational control</strong>. When integrated with <strong>Six Sigma methodologies</strong>, MORT becomes a key tool for <strong>risk management, process improvement, and systematic incident analysis</strong>.</p>

<p>This article delves into the <strong>structure of MORT</strong>, explaining its logic, key components, and practical applications for organizations aiming to <strong>reduce risk and enhance operational reliability</strong>.</p>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">The Top Event: Losses</h3>

<p>At the heart of the MORT framework is the <strong>top event</strong>, labeled <strong>“Losses”</strong>. Every analysis begins by identifying the undesired outcome—be it injury, equipment damage, environmental harm, or financial loss.</p>

<p>Below “Losses” are the <strong>two primary branches</strong>:</p>

<ol class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Oversights and Omissions</strong>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Represents failures in managing and controlling work activities.</li>

<li>Input to this branch occurs through an <strong>AND logic gate</strong>, emphasizing that problems often involve multiple management processes working in combination.</li>
</ul>
</li>

<li><strong>Assumed Risks</strong>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Accounts for losses that arise from <strong>risks knowingly accepted and managed</strong>.</li>

<li>Only factors transferred to this branch are considered assumed risks; otherwise, all are treated as oversights and omissions.</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ol>

<p>By distinguishing between oversights and assumed risks, MORT ensures that investigations <strong>focus on preventable failures first</strong>, rather than risks that were properly assessed and deliberately accepted.</p>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Specific and Management Control Branches</h3>

<p>The <strong>Oversights and Omissions branch</strong> is further divided into two critical sub-branches:</p>

<ol class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Specific Control Factors (SA1 &amp; SA2)</strong>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>SA1:</strong> Factors directly related to the incident or accident itself.</li>

<li><strong>SA2:</strong> Factors related to <strong>restoring control after an accident</strong>, such as emergency response or mitigation efforts.</li>

<li>These two are linked via an <strong>OR gate</strong>, meaning that either can contribute to losses.</li>
</ul>
</li>

<li><strong>Management Control Factors</strong>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Reflect deficiencies in <strong>planning, supervision, policy, and other management processes</strong>.</li>

<li>These highlight areas where organizational oversight failed, allowing unsafe conditions or processes to persist.</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ol>

<p>By breaking down MORT into <strong>specific and management control branches</strong>, organizations gain a clear understanding of <strong>both operational and systemic factors</strong> contributing to losses.</p>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">The MORT Diagram: Levels and Complexity</h3>

<p>MORT analysis is implemented using <strong>MORT diagrams</strong>, which vary in size and detail depending on the level of investigation required:</p>

<ol class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Basic Diagram</strong>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Approximately <strong>300 blocks</strong>.</li>

<li>Suitable for <strong>hazard identification checks</strong> and basic risk awareness.</li>
</ul>
</li>

<li><strong>Intermediate Diagram</strong>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Around <strong>1,500 blocks</strong>.</li>

<li>Used for more detailed investigations into <strong>moderate risk events</strong>.</li>
</ul>
</li>

<li><strong>Comprehensive Diagram</strong>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>About <strong>10,000 blocks</strong>, filling an entire book.</li>

<li>Reserved for <strong>high-risk or mission-critical activities</strong> due to the time and resources required.</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ol>

<p>Analysts can <strong>tailor diagrams</strong> to focus on specific branches of interest, making the tool flexible yet systematic. The process always begins by <strong>placing the undesired loss at the top</strong>, then working through the diagram to identify <strong>“less than adequate” blocks</strong> that require risk control actions.</p>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">How MORT Works in Practice</h3>

<ol class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Systematic Assessment</strong>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Analysts respond to each issue posed by the MORT diagram.</li>

<li><strong>Blocks that fail or are less than adequate</strong> are highlighted for corrective action.</li>
</ul>
</li>

<li><strong>Negative Tree Approach</strong>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>MORT is essentially a <strong>negative tree</strong>, starting with the undesired outcome and tracing backward to reveal <strong>contributing factors</strong>.</li>
</ul>
</li>

<li><strong>Professional Expertise Required</strong>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Full application is best performed by <strong>trained loss control professionals</strong>.</li>

<li>Even a basic MORT diagram can support <strong>hazard identification and process evaluation</strong> if used thoughtfully.</li>
</ul>
</li>

<li><strong>Integration with Six Sigma</strong>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>MORT complements Six Sigma tools like <strong>DMAIC</strong> by providing <strong>root cause identification, management oversight evaluation, and barrier analysis</strong>.</li>

<li>Using MORT in Six Sigma initiatives helps <strong>reduce variability, prevent losses, and improve overall process control</strong>.</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ol>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Benefits of Understanding MORT Structure</h3>

<ol class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Comprehensive Risk Identification</strong>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>MORT helps organizations <strong>see both operational failures and management deficiencies</strong>, ensuring a complete picture of risk.</li>
</ul>
</li>

<li><strong>Prioritized Risk Control</strong>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Highlighting “less than adequate” blocks allows <strong>focused corrective action</strong>, improving efficiency and safety outcomes.</li>
</ul>
</li>

<li><strong>Scalability</strong>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Organizations can <strong>tailor MORT diagrams</strong> based on risk level, resource availability, and investigation scope.</li>
</ul>
</li>

<li><strong>Enhanced Incident Investigation</strong>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>The structured approach ensures that <strong>all contributing factors are systematically examined</strong>, increasing confidence in findings.</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ol>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Conclusion</h3>

<p>The <strong>Management Oversight and Risk Tree (MORT)</strong> is a powerful tool for organizations committed to <strong>risk reduction, operational excellence, and process safety</strong>. Its structured framework—dividing causes into <strong>oversights, omissions, and assumed risks</strong>, and further into <strong>specific and management control factors</strong>—ensures that investigations are thorough, logical, and actionable.</p>

<p>When applied within a <strong>Six Sigma framework</strong>, MORT not only enhances <strong>incident investigations</strong> but also strengthens <strong>process controls, barrier management, and systemic risk reduction</strong>, making it indispensable for industries where <strong>safety, reliability, and operational excellence</strong> are non-negotiable.</p>

<p>By understanding MORT’s structure and effectively applying its diagrams, organizations can <strong>proactively identify risk, implement corrective measures, and safeguard their most critical assets</strong>.</p>
