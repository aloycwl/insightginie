<p><em>Fusobacterium nucleatum</em> (Fn) is a gram-negative anaerobe increasingly recognized for its role in <strong>periodontal disease, colorectal cancer, adverse pregnancy outcomes, and systemic infections</strong>. Its ability to colonize and form biofilms in various tissues, modulate immune responses, and co-localize with tumor cells has made it a critical target for therapeutic intervention. However, the <strong>lack of Fn-specific antibiotics</strong> and rising antimicrobial resistance pose challenges in its management. One promising strategy to address this is <strong>drug repositioning</strong>, also known as drug repurposing. This approach involves identifying new uses for existing, approved drugs to combat emerging health threats like Fn. By bypassing early-stage drug development, repositioning offers a <strong>faster, cost-effective</strong> path to clinical solutions.</p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading">What is Drug Repositioning and Why is it Important for Fn?</h3>



<p>Drug repositioning involves taking a <strong>known drug with established safety profiles</strong> and testing its efficacy in treating conditions outside its original indication. The benefits include:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Lower development costs and risks</strong></li>



<li><strong>Faster approval timelines</strong></li>



<li><strong>Pre-existing pharmacokinetics and safety data</strong></li>
</ul>



<p>For <em>Fusobacterium nucleatum</em>, this strategy is particularly useful because:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Fn lacks specific therapeutic agents</li>



<li>Its association with cancer and chronic infections is newly understood</li>



<li>Biofilm-forming bacteria like Fn are hard to treat with conventional antibiotics</li>
</ul>



<p>Using repositioned drugs allows researchers to <strong>target bacterial virulence, colonization, or tumor-promoting effects</strong>, often by mechanisms not originally intended.</p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Promising Repositioned Drugs Against <em>Fusobacterium nucleatum</em></h3>



<h4 class="wp-block-heading">1. <strong>Metronidazole – A Traditional Antibiotic Revisited</strong></h4>



<p>While widely used against anaerobic bacteria, <strong>metronidazole</strong> is being re-examined for its activity against Fn in specific disease contexts, particularly in <strong>colorectal cancer</strong>. Some studies show that Fn-positive CRC patients may benefit from <strong>targeted metronidazole regimens</strong>, especially in reducing tumor-promoting inflammation.</p>



<h4 class="wp-block-heading">2. <strong>Aspirin – Beyond Cardioprotection</strong></h4>



<p>Low-dose aspirin has been shown to <strong>modulate inflammation and reduce cancer risk</strong>, but recent research reveals that it may also <strong>lower Fn abundance</strong> in colorectal tissues. A 2020 <em>Cancer Research</em> study found that aspirin could inhibit Fn-induced oncogenic pathways like β-catenin signaling, suggesting it may help <strong>limit Fn-related tumor progression</strong>.</p>



<h4 class="wp-block-heading">3. <strong>Statins – Targeting Bacterial Invasion</strong></h4>



<p>Statins such as <strong>simvastatin and atorvastatin</strong>, known for cholesterol-lowering effects, have <strong>antibacterial and anti-inflammatory properties</strong>. Evidence suggests they may <strong>reduce Fn adherence and invasion</strong> in epithelial cells, while also dampening inflammation, which is key in Fn-associated tumorigenesis.</p>



<h4 class="wp-block-heading">4. <strong>Disulfiram – An Anti-Alcoholism Drug with Antibiofilm Action</strong></h4>



<p>Disulfiram, approved for alcohol aversion therapy, has shown <strong>biofilm-disrupting activity</strong> against various pathogens. Emerging data suggests it may impair Fn’s <strong>biofilm integrity and virulence gene expression</strong>, making it a compelling candidate in oral health and colorectal cancer contexts.</p>



<h4 class="wp-block-heading">5. <strong>Chloroquine – Autophagy Modulator with Anti-Fn Potential</strong></h4>



<p>Chloroquine, traditionally used for malaria and autoimmune diseases, can <strong>modulate host autophagy pathways</strong>. Since Fn can exploit host autophagy to survive, chloroquine may counteract this by <strong>disrupting bacterial persistence</strong> in cancerous tissues.</p>



<h4 class="wp-block-heading">6. <strong>Lithocholic Acid (LCA) and Bile Acids</strong></h4>



<p>Repositioned bile acid derivatives have shown the ability to <strong>inhibit Fn growth and biofilm formation</strong>. LCA interferes with bacterial membrane integrity and may be useful as a <strong>gut microbiome modulator</strong> in Fn-dominant environments.</p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Mechanisms by Which Repositioned Drugs Affect Fn</h3>



<p>The success of repositioned drugs often lies in their <strong>unexpected multi-targeted effects</strong>, which can include:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Inhibition of biofilm formation</strong></li>



<li><strong>Disruption of bacterial adhesion molecules</strong> (e.g., FadA, Fap2)</li>



<li><strong>Modulation of immune responses</strong>, particularly IL-6 and TNF-α</li>



<li><strong>Interference with host-pathogen signaling pathways</strong></li>



<li><strong>Synergistic action with antibiotics</strong>, enhancing their potency</li>
</ul>



<p>This <strong>mechanistic versatility</strong> enables these drugs to tackle Fn both directly and indirectly.</p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Drug Repositioning in Clinical and Research Contexts</h3>



<p>Several ongoing research initiatives and trials are focusing on drug repositioning for microbiota-related diseases. Fn is now recognized as a <strong>targetable microbe</strong> in the tumor microenvironment, particularly in <strong>colorectal and head and neck cancers</strong>. Repositioned drugs are being explored in:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Adjunctive cancer therapies</strong></li>



<li><strong>Oral health products (e.g., statin-containing mouthwashes)</strong></li>



<li><strong>Gut microbiota management via bile acids and dietary compounds</strong></li>



<li><strong>Perioperative treatments to reduce Fn-driven complications</strong></li>
</ul>



<p>Drug repositioning also offers a bridge between <strong>oncology, microbiology, and immunology</strong>, opening up new interdisciplinary treatment paradigms.</p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Conclusion: A Strategic Shortcut to Combat <em>Fusobacterium nucleatum</em></h3>



<p>As research continues to unveil the diverse roles of <em>Fusobacterium nucleatum</em> in disease, <strong>drug repositioning stands out as a practical and powerful strategy</strong>. It enables scientists and clinicians to rapidly deploy existing drugs in new contexts, saving time and resources while targeting Fn more effectively. From metronidazole and aspirin to statins and bile acids, the arsenal of repositioned drugs offers <strong>promising avenues for infection control, cancer support, and microbiome balance</strong>. As clinical trials and translational studies expand, repositioned therapies may soon become <strong>mainstream in managing Fn-related conditions</strong>, reinforcing the value of innovation through reapplication.</p>
