Imagine a technology touted as a revolutionary leap in non-invasive health monitoring—capable of reading vital signs through the skin with just a camera. Transdermal optical imaging (TOI) promises this futuristic vision, yet its real-world performance tells a far more complicated story. While lab conditions paint an optimistic picture, the **limitations of transdermal optical imaging in diverse real-world environments** reveal critical gaps that could undermine its reliability, accessibility, and even its ethical deployment.

TOI's allure lies in its simplicity: no wearables, no invasive procedures, just a camera analyzing light absorption to detect blood flow, heart rate, or stress levels. But what happens when that camera faces the unpredictability of real life? From varying skin tones to ambient lighting, motion artifacts to environmental noise, the technology's fragility becomes impossible to ignore. This isn't just a technical hiccup—it's a fundamental challenge that demands scrutiny.
