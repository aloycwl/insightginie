Anxiety and stress-related disorders are among the most common mental health issues globally, affecting hundreds of millions of people each year. Traditional methods of diagnosis—clinical interviews, self-report questionnaires, and psychological assessments—often capture only snapshots of an individual's mental health and are limited by subjectivity, recall bias, and infrequent sampling.

Enter **digital phenotyping**, a revolutionary approach that collects and analyzes real-time behavioral data from digital devices such as smartphones, wearables, and sensors. This data allows for continuous, passive monitoring of mental health, enabling early detection and intervention. By tapping into **behavioral signals**—such as movement patterns, typing speed, voice tone, and physiological responses—digital phenotyping offers a powerful new way to identify and track **anxiety** and **stress** in daily life.

This article explores how digital phenotyping detects anxiety and stress through behavioral signals, discusses the benefits and challenges of this approach, and highlights its potential to reshape modern mental health care.
