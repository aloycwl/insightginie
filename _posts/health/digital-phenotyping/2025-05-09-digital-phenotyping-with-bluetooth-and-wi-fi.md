In the digital age, smartphones are more than communication tools—they are behavioral sensors capable of capturing nuanced human interactions. Among the most promising applications of this capability is **digital phenotyping**, which involves the passive collection and analysis of data to quantify behavioral and physiological patterns. While GPS and accelerometer data capture movement and mobility, **Bluetooth and Wi-Fi signals** provide a powerful lens into **social proximity and interaction metrics**, offering valuable insights into social behavior, loneliness, mental health, and more.

This article delves into how Bluetooth and Wi-Fi signals are used in digital phenotyping to assess interpersonal proximity, social connectedness, and group dynamics, and the growing relevance of this approach in healthcare, research, and epidemiology.
