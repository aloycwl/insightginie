Suicide remains a leading cause of death worldwide, with over 700,000 individuals taking their lives each year, according to the World Health Organization. Traditional methods of suicide risk assessment—clinical interviews, self-report questionnaires, and behavioral observation—often fall short due to stigma, underreporting, and infrequent contact with healthcare providers.

In response to these challenges, **digital phenotyping** has emerged as a powerful new approach. By passively collecting and analyzing behavioral data from smartphones and wearable devices, digital phenotyping allows for the real-time, continuous monitoring of individuals at risk—potentially identifying early warning signs of suicidal ideation before they escalate into crises.

This article explores how digital footprints—ranging from sleep patterns and phone usage to mobility and social interactions—can be used to improve suicide risk assessment and prevention strategies through the lens of digital phenotyping.
