“I just can’t believe that humans evolved from bacteria. It’s too complicated. Therefore, evolution must be false.”

“How can the earth be spinning at 1,000 miles per hour if I don’t feel the motion? It doesn’t make sense, so the earth must be flat.”

We have all heard arguments like these. They appeal to our “gut instinct” or “common sense.” They rely on the idea that if a concept is too difficult to visualize or explain simply, it must be untrue.

This is the **Personal Incredulity Fallacy** (also known as the *Argument from Incredulity*).

It is a logical trap that equates a lack of understanding with a lack of truth. In an increasingly complex world—dominated by quantum computing, mRNA vaccines, and algorithmic economics—this fallacy has become a major barrier to scientific literacy and rational discourse.

To be a high-level thinker, you must accept a humbling reality: The universe is under no obligation to make sense to you.

What is the Personal Incredulity Fallacy?
-----------------------------------------

The fallacy occurs when someone asserts that a proposition must be false because they personally find it difficult to understand or imagine how it could be true.

The logical structure, which is fundamentally flawed, looks like this:

1. **Premise:** I cannot imagine how **X** could be true.
2. **Conclusion:** Therefore, **X** is false.

This is a specific form of the *Argument from Ignorance*. Instead of relying on evidence to disprove a claim, the arguer relies on their own cognitive limitations. It treats the individual’s imagination as the upper limit of reality.

The Trap of “Common Sense”
--------------------------

The primary fuel for personal incredulity is “common sense.” We evolve to understand the world on a macro, human scale. We understand throwing a rock, walking a mile, or lighting a fire.

However, reality operates on scales that are far outside human intuition:

* **The Micro:** Quantum entanglement allows particles to affect one another instantly across vast distances. This violates “common sense,” yet it is a proven fact.
* **The Macro:** The sheer scale of geological time (billions of years) is impossible for the human brain to truly comprehend.
* **The Complex:** Global economic systems involve billions of variables interacting simultaneously.

When we try to judge these complex realities using “common sense” evolved for hunting and gathering, we fail. The Personal Incredulity Fallacy is the brain’s defense mechanism against that failure. It rejects the complex reality to preserve the simple worldview.

Classic Examples in the Wild
----------------------------

To spot this fallacy, look for phrases like “It seems impossible that…” or “I just don’t see how…”

### 1. The Argument Against Evolution

This is the most famous arena for this fallacy.

* *The Argument:* “The human eye is so perfect and complex. I cannot imagine how it could have formed by random mutations over time. It’s impossible.”
* *The Reality:* The fact that the arguer cannot “imagine” the millions of years of gradual steps (from light-sensitive cells to cupped eyes to lensed eyes) is a failure of their imagination, not a failure of evolutionary biology. The evidence exists, regardless of whether the person can visualize the timeline.

### 2. Conspiracy Theories

Many conspiracies are born from incredulity regarding competence or coincidence.

* *The Argument:* “I can’t believe a lone gunman could shoot a President with such accuracy. It’s too unbelievable. It must be a massive government plot.”
* *The Reality:* Improbable events happen. High-stress performance varies. The inability to accept that a single individual can change history drives the need for a more complex (but comfortable) narrative.

### 3. Climate Science

* *The Argument:* “It’s freezing outside today! I don’t see how the planet can be warming up.”
* *The Reality:* The individual is confusing local weather with global climate. Their personal experience (feeling cold) makes them incredulous toward the statistical reality of global average temperatures.

The “God of the Gaps” Connection
--------------------------------

Personal Incredulity is often the precursor to the **Divine Fallacy** (or God of the Gaps). When a person cannot understand a natural phenomenon, they often insert a supernatural explanation to fill the void.

* “I don’t understand how lightning works; therefore, Zeus must be throwing bolts.”
* “I don’t understand how the Big Bang happened; therefore, a deity must have snapped their fingers.”

As scientific knowledge expands, the “gaps” where we insert these supernatural explanations get smaller. The fallacy lies in assuming that an *currently* unexplained mystery is *unexplainable* by natural means.

Why We Fall for It (The Psychology)
-----------------------------------

Why is it so hard to admit we don’t understand something?

### 1. Cognitive Ease

Thinking is metabolically expensive. Assessing complex evidence requires effort. Dismissing an idea because it “sounds crazy” is easy. It saves mental energy.

### 2. Ego Protection

Admitting “I don’t understand this” requires humility. It admits a lack of knowledge. Saying “This is false” allows the person to feel superior. It reframes their ignorance as “skepticism.”

How to Overcome Personal Incredulity
------------------------------------

If you want to ensure your decisions are based on facts rather than feelings, you must learn to bypass your own incredulity.

### 1. Distinguish “Counter-Intuitive” from “False”

Acknowledge that truth is often counter-intuitive. Airplanes are heavy metal tubes that fly. We are made of stardust. The internet travels through glass cables under the ocean. None of this makes “sense” intuitively, but it is all true.

### 2. Do the Work (Research)

If you find yourself saying, “I don’t see how that works,” treat it as a prompt to learn, not a prompt to deny.  
Read the study. Watch the lecture. If you don’t understand the mechanism of mRNA vaccines, read a biologist’s explanation before declaring them impossible.

### 3. Trust the Consensus (The Proxy Method)

You cannot be an expert in everything. If 99% of neuroscientists agree on how the brain works, and you (an accountant) think it sounds wrong, the probability is high that *you* are the one missing information.

Conclusion
----------

The Personal Incredulity Fallacy is the enemy of progress. It anchors us to the past, limiting our understanding to what was “sensible” to our ancestors.

The next time you are confronted with a new idea, a complex scientific theory, or a strange statistical fact, pause. Do not let your confusion morph into denial. It is perfectly acceptable to say, “I don’t understand that.” It is a logical error to add, “…so it must be wrong.”

Embrace the complexity. The world is far more interesting than our “common sense” gives it credit for.
