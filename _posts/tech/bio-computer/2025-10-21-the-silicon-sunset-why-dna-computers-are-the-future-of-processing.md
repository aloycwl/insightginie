For decades, silicon chips have been the undisputed kings of the digital realm. Powering everything from our smartphones to supercomputers, these tiny marvels have driven an unprecedented era of technological advancement, famously encapsulated by Moore's Law. Yet, as we push the boundaries of physics and engineering, the silicon era is showing signs of strain. The relentless pursuit of smaller, faster, and more powerful processors is running into fundamental limits: heat dissipation, energy consumption, and the sheer impossibility of shrinking transistors much further.

But what if the future of computing doesn't lie in ever-smaller etched circuits, but in the very building blocks of life itself? Imagine computers that operate not with electricity, but with molecular reactions; machines that store data not on magnetic platters, but within the double helix of DNA. This isn't science fiction. This is the burgeoning field of DNA computing, and it holds the potential to revolutionize how we process information, perhaps even replacing traditional silicon chips entirely.
