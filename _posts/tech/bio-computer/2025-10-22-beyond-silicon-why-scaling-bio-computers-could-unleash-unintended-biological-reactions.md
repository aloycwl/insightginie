Imagine computers that grow, self-repair, and process information with the elegance and efficiency of life itself. This isn't science fiction; it's the promise of bio-computing, a revolutionary field that seeks to harness biological molecules and systems – like DNA and proteins – to perform computational tasks. From solving complex mathematical problems to targeted drug delivery, the potential applications are breathtaking. Yet, as we stand on the precipice of scaling these incredible biological machines, a critical question looms large: are we prepared for the unintended biological reactions they might trigger?

For decades, our digital world has been built on silicon. But silicon has its limits. Biological systems, by contrast, offer unparalleled parallelism, energy efficiency, and information density. Bio-computers, in their simplest forms, already exist, performing computations within test tubes or even living cells. However, the true power of this technology will only be unlocked when we can scale these systems to rival, or even surpass, conventional electronics. This ambition, while noble, introduces a Pandora's Box of biological complexities that demand our immediate and rigorous attention.
