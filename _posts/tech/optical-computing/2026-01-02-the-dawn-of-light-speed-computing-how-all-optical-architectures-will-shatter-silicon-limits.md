For decades, silicon has been the undisputed king of computing. From our smartphones to supercomputers, the humble electron, flowing through silicon transistors, has powered the digital revolution. Yet, as we push the boundaries of data processing, artificial intelligence, and scientific discovery, the limitations of silicon are becoming increasingly apparent. Moore's Law, the decades-long prediction that the number of transistors on a microchip doubles approximately every two years, is showing signs of slowing. Heat dissipation, power consumption, and the inherent speed limits of electrons are creating bottlenecks that traditional electronics struggle to overcome. But what if we could move beyond electrons entirely? What if we could compute using light?

Enter **all-optical computing architectures** – a revolutionary paradigm that promises to transcend the physical constraints of silicon by harnessing the unparalleled speed and efficiency of photons. This isn't just an incremental improvement; it's a fundamental shift in how we process information, potentially unlocking a future of computational power previously confined to science fiction.
