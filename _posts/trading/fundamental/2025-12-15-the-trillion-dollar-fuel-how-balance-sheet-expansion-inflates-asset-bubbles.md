In the modern financial era, the old rules of supply and demand have been complicated by a massive, invisible hand: the Central Bank. For decades, investors analyzed corporate earnings, GDP growth, and innovation to determine the value of an asset. Today, however, a single metric often overrides all others: the size of the Federal Reserve's balance sheet.

When stock markets disconnect from economic reality—rising to all-time highs while unemployment spikes or manufacturing slows—the culprit is usually **balance sheet expansion**. This mechanism, often referred to as “printing money” or Quantitative Easing (QE), acts as a high-octane fuel for asset prices. But like any artificial stimulant, it comes with a dangerous side effect: the creation of unsustainable asset bubbles.

To navigate today's markets, investors must understand the mechanics of this liquidity firehose, how it distorts valuations, and the inevitable volatility that occurs when the flow stops.
