<h1>Schwab Fundamental U.S. Broad Market ETF (FDTL): A Deep Dive into a Low‑Cost Fundamental Index Fund</h1>
<p>Investors seeking a blend of broad market exposure and a rules‑based, fundamentals‑driven approach often turn to the Schwab Fundamental U.S. Broad Market ETF. With ticker symbol FDTL, this exchange‑traded fund aims to replicate the performance of the Russell RAFI U.S. Broad Market Index, which weights constituent stocks by fundamental measures such as book value, cash flow, sales, and dividends rather than pure market capitalization. In this article we explore the fund’s structure, strategy, performance characteristics, cost advantages, and how it fits into a modern portfolio.</p>
<h2>Understanding Fundamental Indexing</h2>
<p>Traditional market‑cap weighted indexes give the largest companies the biggest influence on index performance. Fundamental indexing, pioneered by Robert Arnott and the Research Affiliates team, reweights stocks based on economic size. The Schwab Fundamental U.S. Broad Market ETF follows this philosophy by assigning weights according to four fundamental factors:</p>
<ul>
<li>Adjusted book value</li>
<li>Cash flow</li>
<li>Sales/revenue</li>
<li>Dividends</li>
</ul>
<p>Each factor is normalized and combined to produce a composite score that determines a stock’s weight in the index. This approach tends to tilt the portfolio toward value‑oriented, financially stable companies while reducing the overweight of expensive, high‑growth stocks that can dominate cap‑weighted benchmarks.</p>

<h2>Cost Efficiency and Tax Considerations</h2>
<p>Schwab’s commitment to low‑cost investing is evident in FDTL’s expense ratio of just 0.25%. Additionally, the ETF structure provides tax efficiency:</p>
<ul>

<li>Quarterly dividends are qualified for most shareholders.</li>
<li>Low turnover reduces realized gains.</li>
</ul>
<p>Investors holding FDTL in a taxable account can therefore expect a relatively modest tax drag compared with higher‑turnover funds.</p>
