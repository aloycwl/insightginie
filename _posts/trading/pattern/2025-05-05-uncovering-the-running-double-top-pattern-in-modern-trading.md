<p>In technical analysis, the <strong>Double Top</strong> is a well-known reversal pattern signaling a shift from bullish to bearish sentiment. However, not all double tops are created equal. The <strong>Running Double Top</strong> adds a unique dimension—it occurs in fast-moving markets where the second peak marginally exceeds the first before a strong reversal. Recognizing and properly trading this variation requires a nuanced understanding of price action and momentum.</p>



<p>In this guide, we’ll break down the <strong>Running Double Top</strong>, highlight its distinctive features, and provide actionable strategies to help traders confidently spot and trade this dynamic reversal pattern.</p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>What Is a Running Double Top Pattern?</strong></h3>



<p>A <strong>Running Double Top</strong> is a variant of the traditional double top, characterized by a <strong>second peak that slightly exceeds the first high</strong>, rather than matching it precisely. This subtle breakout often <strong>traps breakout buyers</strong> before the market swiftly reverses—hence the term &#8220;running.&#8221; The price action is often deceptive, giving the appearance of trend continuation before flipping to the downside.</p>



<p><strong>Key Characteristics:</strong></p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Two peaks</strong>: The second high exceeds the first slightly (a “fake breakout”).</li>



<li><strong>Neckline support</strong>: Price pulls back to a common support level between the peaks.</li>



<li><strong>False breakout trap</strong>: Momentum stalls after the second high, leading to a sharp sell-off.</li>



<li><strong>Increased volume or divergence</strong>: Often accompanied by <strong>bearish RSI/MACD divergence</strong> or low breakout volume.</li>
</ul>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Why the Running Double Top Matters for Traders</strong></h3>



<p>The running variation is significant because it highlights <strong>market psychology</strong>—specifically the <strong>exhaustion of buyers</strong> and the <strong>aggressiveness of sellers</strong> following a fake breakout. It’s a valuable pattern for:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Catching high-probability <strong>reversal trades</strong></li>



<li><strong>Trapping late buyers</strong> at the top of the trend</li>



<li>Managing risk through clearly defined <strong>entry and stop-loss levels</strong></li>
</ul>



<p>This pattern is especially effective in <strong>volatile markets</strong> like Forex, cryptocurrencies, and high-growth tech stocks.</p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Real-World Example: Running Double Top in Action</strong></h3>



<p>Let’s consider a common example in the <strong>cryptocurrency market</strong>:</p>



<h4 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Bitcoin (BTC/USD) Example</strong></h4>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>First peak forms at $48,000</li>



<li>A brief retracement down to $45,500</li>



<li>Price surges to $48,500 (second top), triggering breakout traders</li>



<li>Momentum fades, RSI shows divergence</li>



<li>Price sharply reverses and breaks the neckline at $45,500</li>



<li>Traders enter short positions, targeting $43,000 or lower</li>
</ul>



<p>This scenario shows how the <strong>running double top</strong> traps late buyers, reverses aggressively, and rewards early bears with strong follow-through.</p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>How to Trade the Running Double Top Pattern</strong></h3>



<h4 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>1. Spot the First Peak and Pullback</strong></h4>



<p>Track the initial rally and identify a clear retracement. Draw a horizontal <strong>neckline</strong> connecting the bottom of the retracement.</p>



<h4 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>2. Watch for the Second Top (and False Breakout)</strong></h4>



<p>Once the price exceeds the first peak marginally, stay alert. If <strong>momentum is weaker</strong>, or indicators diverge, it signals a possible fake-out.</p>



<h4 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>3. Wait for Confirmation (Neckline Break)</strong></h4>



<p>Enter a <strong>short position</strong> after the neckline breaks. This confirms the failure of the breakout and validates the pattern.</p>



<h4 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>4. Set Entry, Stop, and Target</strong></h4>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Entry:</strong> On neckline break</li>



<li><strong>Stop-loss:</strong> Just above the second peak</li>



<li><strong>Target:</strong> Measure the height from the peaks to the neckline and project downward</li>
</ul>



<h4 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>5. Use Divergence and Volume</strong></h4>



<p>Bearish divergence in RSI or MACD, or weak volume on the second peak, can strengthen the signal.</p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Best Practices and Pro Tips</strong></h3>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Avoid early entries: Wait for neckline break to confirm the pattern.</li>



<li>Use candlestick confirmation: Look for bearish engulfing or reversal candles near the second peak.</li>



<li>Combine with Fibonacci: Second peaks often align with the 127.2% or 161.8% extensions of the first peak-to-valley leg.</li>
</ul>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Conclusion: Running Double Tops as a Tool for Tactical Traders</strong></h3>



<p>The <strong>Running Double Top</strong> pattern is a powerful variation that traps the unwary and rewards the prepared. By understanding its structure, psychology, and indicators, traders can capitalize on quick reversals in overheated markets. It&#8217;s a dynamic tool to add to any trader’s technical analysis playbook—especially when market momentum begins to fade.</p>
