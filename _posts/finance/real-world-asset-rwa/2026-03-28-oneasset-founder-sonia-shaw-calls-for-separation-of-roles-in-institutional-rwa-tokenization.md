<h1>OneAsset Founder Sonia Shaw Calls for Separation of Roles in Institutional RWA Tokenization</h1>
<p>In recent months, the conversation around real‑world asset (RWA) tokenization has shifted from experimental pilots to serious institutional adoption. Founders, regulators, and investors are all weighing how to bring traditional assets onto blockchain networks while preserving trust and compliance. Sonia Shaw, founder of OneAsset, has emerged as a leading voice urging the industry to adopt a clear separation of functions. She argues that bundling asset origination, token issuance, and market operations within a single entity creates conflicts of interest, obscures risk, and hampers regulatory clarity. This article explores Shaw’s proposal, outlines the benefits of distinct roles, and provides practical steps for institutions looking to implement this model.</p>
<h2>The Current Landscape of Real‑World Asset Tokenization</h2>
<p>Today, a growing number of financial institutions are experimenting with tokenizing assets such as real estate, commodities, private equity, and even intellectual property. The promise is clear: fractional ownership, 24/7 trading, reduced settlement times, and access to a global investor base. However, the early wave of projects has revealed recurring challenges. Many initiatives combine the responsibilities of sourcing the underlying asset, minting the tokens, and providing secondary market liquidity. This concentration of power can lead to asymmetric information, where insiders know more about the asset quality than external participants. Moreover, regulators often struggle to determine which entity is responsible for disclosures, custodianship, and compliance with securities laws.</p>
<p>The market size for tokenized RWAs is projected to exceed $5 trillion by 2030, according to several industry analysts. This explosive growth is driven by the need for more efficient capital markets, especially in sectors where traditional financing is costly or slow. Yet, without proper governance, the rapid influx of capital could amplify systemic vulnerabilities. Shaw warns that the industry must learn from the lessons of traditional securitization, where lax oversight and conflated responsibilities contributed to the 2008 financial crisis.</p>
<h3>Why Institutions Are Flocking to RWA Tokens</h3>
<p>Institutions are attracted to RWA tokenization for several strategic reasons. First, tokenization can unlock liquidity in traditionally illiquid markets, allowing banks and asset managers to rebalance portfolios more efficiently. Second, the transparency of blockchain ledgers offers an auditable trail of ownership transfers, which can reduce fraud and improve due diligence. Third, smart contracts enable programmable features such as automatic dividend distributions, escrow releases, and compliance checks. Finally, the global nature of public blockchains opens doors to cross‑border investment without the need for multiple intermediaries. These benefits have driven a surge in proof‑of‑concept projects, but they have also highlighted the need for robust operational frameworks.</p>
<p>Additionally, tokenized assets can serve as collateral in decentralized finance (DeFi) lending protocols, creating new yield opportunities. For example, a tokenized office building could be used to secure a loan on a platform like Aave, with interest payments encoded directly into the smart contract. This composability amplifies the utility of each tokenized asset, but it also increases the importance of ensuring that the underlying asset is genuinely backed and legally enforceable.</p>
<h3>Common Pitfalls in the Existing Model</h3>
<p>Despite the excitement, several pitfalls have emerged when a single entity handles multiple functions. One major issue is the potential for self‑dealing, where the token issuer may favor its own assets or provide preferential pricing to affiliated parties. Another concern is the lack of clear accountability when something goes wrong; if the token loses value, it is unclear whether the fault lies with the asset originator, the smart‑contract developer, or the market operator. Additionally, combining roles can create barriers to entry for specialized service providers, stifling innovation. Finally, regulators often view vertically integrated models as higher risk, which can lead to delayed approvals or restrictive licensing requirements.</p>
<p>A case in point is a recent tokenized commodity fund that experienced a sharp price drop after an audit revealed discrepancies between the reported warehouse inventory and the tokens in circulation. Because the same firm managed the warehouse, issued the tokens, and operated the trading platform, investors had no independent verification mechanism. The episode resulted in legal actions and a loss of confidence that could have been mitigated with clearer role separation.</p>
<h2>Sonia Shaw’s Core Argument: Separation of Roles</h2>
<p>Shaw’s central thesis is simple yet powerful: the three core functions of an RWA tokenization ecosystem—asset origination and custody, token issuance and smart‑contract development, and market operation and liquidity provision—should be performed by legally and operationally distinct entities. By separating these roles, each participant can focus on its core competency, reduce conflicts of interest, and provide clearer points of accountability. Shaw notes that this mirrors the structure of traditional securitization, where originators, trustees, and servicers operate independently under well‑established legal frameworks. She believes that adopting a similar model in the tokenized world will foster greater trust among regulators, investors, and the broader public.</p>
<p>She further emphasizes that separation does not imply fragmentation; rather, it encourages collaboration through well‑defined interfaces. For instance, asset custodians can provide periodic attestation reports that token issuers rely on to mint or burn tokens. Market operators, in turn, can subscribe to real‑time feeds of token supply and asset valuations to ensure fair pricing. This modular approach enables each layer to innovate independently while maintaining overall system integrity.</p>
<h3>Defining the Three Key Functions</h3>
<p>Each function can be fulfilled by a specialized firm, a consortium, or even a decentralized autonomous organization (DAO) that adheres to clear governance rules. The key is that no single entity controls more than one of these functions, thereby preventing the concentration of power that has plagued early projects.</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Asset Originator and Custodian</strong>: Responsible for sourcing, verifying, and holding the underlying real‑world asset. This entity conducts due diligence, ensures legal title, and provides custodial safeguards such as insurance or third‑party vaulting.</li>
<li><strong>Token Issuer and Smart‑Contract Developer</strong>: Takes the verified asset and creates the digital representation. This party designs the tokenomics, writes and audits the smart contracts, and handles regulatory filings related to the token itself.</li>
<li><strong>Market Operator and Liquidity Provider</strong>: Manages the secondary market where tokens trade. This includes operating exchanges or AMMs, providing liquidity pools, ensuring fair pricing, and enforcing market conduct rules.</li>
</ul>
<h2>Benefits of a Clear Role Separation</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Enhanced Regulatory Clarity</strong>: Regulators can easily identify which entity is responsible for asset backing, token compliance, and market supervision.</li>
<li><strong>Reduced Conflict of Interest</strong>: Separation removes incentives for self‑dealing and promotes arm‑length transactions.</li>
<li><strong>Improved Risk Management</strong>: Risks are isolated to the relevant layer; a problem in the asset layer does not automatically contaminate the token or market layers.</li>
<li><strong>Greater Transparency for Investors</strong>: Investors can review independent reports from each party, making it easier to assess the quality of the underlying asset and the integrity of the token.</li>
<li><strong>Facilitated Innovation Across Layers</strong>: Specialists can compete to improve their specific function—better custody solutions, more efficient smart contracts, or innovative liquidity mechanisms—without being hindered by unrelated responsibilities.</li>
</ul>
<h2>Real‑World Examples and Comparisons</h2>
<h3>Example 1: Traditional Securitization vs. Tokenized RWA</h3>
<p>In a classic mortgage‑backed security (MBS) transaction, the loan originator sells the mortgages to a special purpose vehicle (SPV). The SPV issues the securities, while a trustee monitors compliance and a servicer handles collections. Each party has a distinct legal role, and the structure has survived decades of regulatory scrutiny. By contrast, many early tokenized real‑estate projects have the same company sourcing the property, minting the tokens, and running the trading platform. Shaw points out that this mirrors the risks seen in the pre‑2008 MBS market, where lax oversight and tangled responsibilities contributed to the crisis.</p>
<p>Consider a hypothetical tokenized office building. In the traditional securitization path, a property owner (originator) transfers the deed to a trustee (custodian). The trustee works with a legal counsel to draft the prospectus, while a rating agency evaluates the cash flows. An investment bank then underwrites and sells the securities to investors. In the tokenized analogue, the property owner could engage a specialized custodial service to hold the title, a token issuance platform to create ERC‑3643 tokens representing fractional ownership, and a licensed digital securities exchange to list and trade those tokens. Each party would be subject to its own regulatory oversight, and investors could request attestation reports from the custodian, audit reports from the token issuer, and trade surveillance data from the exchange.</p>
<h3>Example 2: Equity Crowdfunding Platforms</h3>
<p>Platforms such as SeedInvest and Kickstarter separate the fundraising activity from the post‑investment governance. The platform facilitates the offering, but the company retains control over its operations, and investors receive rights through separate agreements. This separation has helped crowdfunding gain legitimacy and attract institutional capital. Tokenized RWAs could adopt a similar approach, where the token issuance platform focuses solely on compliance and smart‑contract delivery, while asset managers handle the underlying equity or debt.</p>
<h3>Example 3: Decentralized Finance (DeFi) Lending Protocols</h3>
<p>Protocols like Aave and Compound clearly delineate the roles of lenders, borrowers, and the protocol itself. The protocol supplies the smart‑contract infrastructure, but it does not originate the loans or dictate collateral quality; those decisions remain with the users. This modular design has allowed rapid innovation while maintaining a high degree of security. Applying this principle to RWA tokenization would mean that token issuers provide the blockchain layer, while specialized custodians and market makers handle the off‑chain components.</p>
<h2>Implementation Steps for Institutions</h2>
<p>By following these steps, institutions can transition from ad‑hoc, vertically integrated pilots to a structured, scalable model that aligns with both innovation goals and regulatory expectations.</p>
<ol>
<li>Conduct a Role‑Mapping Exercise: Identify all current activities and assign them to one of the three layers—asset, token, or market.</li>
<li>Establish Independent Governance Boards: Each layer should have its own oversight body, potentially including independent directors, to monitor compliance and performance.</li>
<li>Adopt Standardized Legal Frameworks: Use existing structures such as SPVs, trusts, or DAOs with clear charters that prohibit cross‑layer ownership beyond a minimal threshold.</li>
<li>Leverage Third‑Party Auditors: Engage external auditors for asset verification, smart‑contract security, and market operation reviews.</li>
<li>Implement Continuous Monitoring and Reporting: Set up real‑time dashboards that show asset backing, token supply, and market liquidity, accessible to regulators and investors.</li>
<li>Develop Interoperability Standards: Define APIs and data formats that allow each layer to exchange information securely, such as proof‑of‑reserve attestations and token supply updates.</li>
<li>Plan for Exit Strategies: Ensure that each layer can unwind its participation without destabilizing the others, for example through predefined redemption procedures or buy‑back agreements.</li>
</ol>
<h2>Potential Challenges and How to Overcome Them</h2>
<p>Addressing these challenges head‑on will allow firms to reap the benefits of role separation without being slowed down by avoidable friction.</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Legacy Systems Integration</strong>: Many banks rely on legacy core systems that are not designed for blockchain interoperability. Solution: Use middleware APIs and cloud‑based orchestration layers that translate between legacy data formats and blockchain protocols.</li>
<li><strong>Coordination Costs</strong>: Working with multiple partners can increase negotiation and management overhead. Solution: Develop standardized service level agreements (SLAs) and shared governance templates to reduce friction.</li>
<li><strong>Regulatory Uncertainty</strong>: Different jurisdictions may treat tokens, assets, and marketplaces differently. Solution: Engage early with regulators, participate in sandbox programs, and adopt a compliance‑by‑design approach.</li>
<li><strong>Technology Standardization</strong>: The lack of universal token standards for RWAs can hinder liquidity. Solution: Support emerging standards such as ERC‑3643 (Permissioned Tokens) or ISO‑20022‑based token representations, and contribute to industry working groups.</li>
<li><strong>Liquidity Fragmentation</strong>: Separating market operation from issuance could lead to fragmented liquidity pools. Solution: Encourage the creation of shared liquidity hubs or federated AMMs that aggregate tokens from multiple issuers while respecting custody guarantees.</li>
</ul>
<h2>Conclusion</h2>
<p>Sonia Shaw’s call for a separation of roles in institutional RWA tokenization is both timely and pragmatic. As the market moves from niche experiments to mainstream adoption, the need for clear accountability, reduced conflicts of interest, and regulatory compatibility becomes paramount. By dividing asset origination, token issuance, and market operation into distinct, specialized functions, the industry can build a foundation that mirrors the robustness of traditional finance while embracing the efficiency and transparency of blockchain technology. Institutions that adopt this model early will not only mitigate risk but also position themselves as leaders in the next wave of digital asset innovation.</p>
<h2>FAQ</h2>
<h3>What is RWA tokenization?</h3>
<p>RWA tokenization is the process of creating a digital token on a blockchain that represents ownership or a claim over a tangible or intangible real‑world asset, such as real estate, commodities, invoices, or equity. The token can be traded, transferred, or used as collateral in a decentralized or regulated environment.</p>
<h3>Why does Sonia Shaw advocate for role separation?</h3>
<p>Shaw argues that combining asset sourcing, token creation, and market operation within a single entity creates conflicts of interest, obscures accountability, and increases systemic risk. Separating these functions mirrors proven structures in traditional securitization and promotes transparency, regulatory clarity, and innovation.</p>
<h3>How does role separation affect investors?</h3>
<p>Investors benefit from clearer visibility into each layer of the tokenization process. They can review independent custodial reports, smart‑contract audits, and market liquidity data, which reduces information asymmetry and helps them make more informed decisions.</p>
<h3>Are there any existing projects that already use this model?</h3>
<p>Several emerging platforms are experimenting with layered approaches. For example, some real‑estate tokenization projects use a trusted custodian for property titles, a separate token issuer for compliance, and a licensed exchange for secondary trading. While still early, these pilots demonstrate the feasibility of the separation model.</p>
<h3>What regulatory bodies are watching this space?</h3>
<p>In the United States, the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) and the Commodity Futures Trading Commission (CFTC) are actively reviewing tokenized assets. In Europe, the European Securities and Markets Authority (ESMA) and individual national regulators are developing frameworks under MiCA. Other jurisdictions such as Singapore, Switzerland, and the UK have also issued guidance on digital securities and tokenized RWAs.</p>
