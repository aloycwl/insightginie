Artificial intelligence is rapidly evolving from a tool that assists with isolated tasks into a powerful ecosystem of autonomous agents capable of executing entire workflows. The groundbreaking report **“Artificial Intelligence Agents and Agentic Workflows: The New Frontier of Automation”** by Juan Gustavo Corvalán and his team at UBA IALAB offers a comprehensive and practical roadmap to understanding this paradigm shift. Published in 2025, the report bridges theory and application, demonstrating how generative AI is redefining automation, productivity, and the balance of power between machines and human oversight.

At its core, agentic AI refers to AI systems that are not just responsive but also proactive, capable of planning, adapting, and executing tasks across dynamic environments with minimal human intervention. These agents differ significantly from traditional automation, which relies on rigid rule-based logic. Instead, agentic AI uses advanced models—especially generative AI and large language models (LLMs)—to interpret context, interact with tools, recall past interactions, and make autonomous decisions.

The evolution of these agents began with simple chatbots like ELIZA and matured through systems like Siri and Alexa. However, the real transformation started with the rise of generative AI, particularly with the launch of ChatGPT. This leap introduced multitasking capabilities, greater autonomy, and accessibility for non-experts. In 2024, the field entered the era of multimodality, enabling AI systems to process and produce outputs across text, image, audio, and video formats. This multimodal sophistication paved the way for agents that can engage in deeper, more versatile interactions.

By 2025, the development of memory features, internet access, and task scheduling marked the arrival of agents that could begin executing full workflows. These “base agents” form the backbone of what is now termed *agentic workflows*. They are no longer just assistants; they are coordinators, decision-makers, and, in some cases, independent executors of complex tasks across business, legal, administrative, and technical domains.

The report introduces a layered framework to classify these agents by autonomy, drawing parallels with autonomous vehicle standards. At the lower levels are tools that automate routine tasks, while at the highest level are agents capable of managing end-to-end processes without human guidance. Importantly, it distinguishes between base agents like GPT-4o, Gemini, and Claude, and *specialized* agents designed for distinct functions such as orchestrating multiple workflows or managing task-specific subtasks.

A key concept introduced is that of **Human Augmented Agents**. While agentic systems are becoming increasingly autonomous, the report emphasizes the necessity of human oversight to correct errors, mitigate hallucinations, and ensure ethical and legal compliance. Human-in-the-loop designs are not just safety mechanisms—they enhance learning and system reliability, fostering trust between users and AI.

The UBA IALAB team also proposes a practical taxonomy for classifying agents into three main categories: base agents, derived (task-specific) agents, and orchestrator agents. Each plays a role within the broader agentic ecosystem. Base agents act as general-purpose engines, derived agents carry out targeted actions often built with no-code or low-code platforms, and orchestrator agents function as intelligent conductors that coordinate multiple agents to ensure seamless execution of complex workflows.

The report warns, however, that agentic AI should not be adopted blindly. Not every workflow requires a sophisticated AI agent. In many scenarios, traditional automation or AI-enhanced workflows with minimal autonomy might be more efficient and cost-effective. The authors stress that selecting the right solution depends on the complexity, adaptability, and risk associated with the use case.

A major takeaway is the transformation of human roles. Users are transitioning from operators and prompt writers to *editors* and *validators*, overseeing the sequences of decisions made by increasingly independent agents. This shift mirrors the growing importance of AI literacy—not just in understanding how to use the tools, but in supervising their actions within ethical and strategic frameworks.

In sum, the UBA IALAB report is a clarion call for organizations to prepare for a future dominated by agentic AI. The path forward is not about replacing humans with machines but designing systems where machines extend human capabilities. This collaboration promises increased efficiency, enhanced decision-making, and unprecedented levels of automation—but only if guided by thoughtful implementation and continuous human oversight.
