In the rapidly evolving world of artificial intelligence and workflow automation, the ability to break down complex problems is arguably the most valuable skill an agent can possess. OpenClaw, a sophisticated framework for managing AI agents, introduces a specialized capability known as the **Divide Agent skill**. This skill is specifically designed to act as a structured thinking partner, ensuring that when you provide a complex task, the agent doesn't just offer an answer, but a deeply organized roadmap of how to approach the problem.

What is the Divide Agent Skill?
-------------------------------

At its core, the Divide Agent skill is an AI-powered module rooted in the **MECE principle (Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive)**. The primary objective of this skill is not to solve the problem itself—rather, it is to decompose the problem into a logical structure that makes solving it manageable, efficient, and thorough. By removing the urge for the AI to jump straight to conclusions, the Divide Agent creates a foundation of sub-problems that covers every aspect of the user's intent without overlaps.

The MECE Framework: A Foundation for Clarity
--------------------------------------------

The Divide Agent is hard-coded to prioritize the MECE principle. To ensure that the decomposition is rigorous, it follows a hierarchy of five specific methodologies:

### 1. Existing Analysis Models (Priority 1)

When the user's problem falls into a known domain, the agent prefers standardized business frameworks. This includes the **Porter's Five Forces** for industry analysis, the **3C model** for strategy, the **7S framework** for organizational alignment, the **4P model** for marketing, and the **PPM Matrix** for portfolio management. By leveraging these established tools, the agent ensures that the decomposition adheres to industry-standard logic rather than reinventing the wheel.

### 2. Element Decomposition

If no formal business model applies, the agent shifts to breaking down the object into its individual constituent elements. By identifying the unique properties of a problem, it can categorize them into distinct buckets, providing a granular view of the situation.

### 3. Process Decomposition

For workflows or tasks that follow a timeline, the agent uses process decomposition. By mapping out the “flow from start point to end point,” it ensures that every step of a project is accounted for in chronological order.

### 4. Symmetric Dichotomy

When binary relationships are present, the agent employs symmetric dichotomy. Think of this as the “A vs B” approach—classifying problems based on polarities like interior vs. exterior, positive vs. negative, or short-term vs. long-term.

### 5. Matrix Method

Finally, for multifaceted problems, the agent constructs a matrix. By selecting two independent variables, it creates a structure that allows for a multidimensional understanding of the subject matter.

The Workflow of the Divide Agent
--------------------------------

The power of the Divide Agent lies in its rigid, reliable workflow. It doesn't just “think”; it executes a specific sequence to ensure output quality:

* **Requirement Analysis:** It starts by absorbing the background information provided by the user.
* **Principle Selection:** It evaluates the five MECE principles to select the most logically sound method for the specific task at hand.
* **Two-Layer Decomposition:** It performs a two-layer breakdown. The first layer sets the scope, and the second layer drills down further, ensuring no second-layer branch has more than three sub-problems. This prevents analysis paralysis.
* **Visual Representation:** The agent uses **Mermaid syntax** to generate a tree diagram. This visual aid is crucial for users to “see” the logical hierarchy of the problem.
* **Documentation and Submission:** The final output is formatted into a wiki-style document, complete with an explanation of the principles used and the rationale behind the decomposition choices.
