Intrapleural fibrinolysis therapy (IPFT) using **alteplase** and **dornase alfa** has become a widely accepted, minimally invasive alternative to surgery for managing **complicated parapneumonic effusions** and empyema. Clinical trials, most notably the MIST2 study, have demonstrated its safety and efficacy in breaking down loculations and improving drainage outcomes.

However, as with any powerful therapeutic intervention, rare but serious complications can occur. Among these, **haemorrhagic pericardial effusion** is emerging as an extremely rare but potentially fatal adverse event. This article explores the clinical context, pathophysiology, case evidence, and critical recommendations for clinicians using IPFT.

---

### Understanding Intrapleural Fibrinolysis Therapy

#### 1. **Mechanism of Action**

In IPFT, **alteplase**, a tissue plasminogen activator (tPA), is used to break down fibrinous septations within the pleural space. **Dornase alfa**, a recombinant human DNase, helps reduce pus viscosity by digesting extracellular DNA released from neutrophils. Together, they facilitate drainage through chest tubes in patients with complex parapneumonic effusions.

#### 2. **Clinical Application**

* Indicated in patients with multiloculated empyema not responding to antibiotics and drainage
* Reduces need for surgical intervention
* Typically involves intrapleural instillation of alteplase (10 mg) and dornase alfa (5 mg), administered via chest tubes for 3 consecutive days

---

### Haemorrhagic Pericardial Effusion: A Rare Complication

#### 1. **Definition and Clinical Significance**

Haemorrhagic pericardial effusion refers to the accumulation of blood within the pericardial sac. If undiagnosed or untreated, it may lead to **cardiac tamponade**, a life-threatening condition resulting from impaired cardiac filling.

#### 2. **Case Evidence**

Though uncommon, case reports have documented haemorrhagic pericardial effusion shortly after the administration of intrapleural alteplase/dornase alfa. In these cases, patients developed:

* Sudden hemodynamic instability
* Hypotension
* Elevated jugular venous pressure
* Diminished heart sounds
* Echocardiographic evidence of pericardial fluid with tamponade physiology

Emergency pericardiocentesis revealed frank blood, confirming the diagnosis.

---

### Potential Mechanisms of Complication

Several hypotheses explain how IPFT may lead to haemorrhagic pericardial effusion:

#### 1. **Anatomical Proximity**

The pleura and pericardium lie in close anatomical relation, especially in the left hemithorax. In cases of severe inflammation or iatrogenic trauma, fibrinolytic agents may track into the pericardial space.

#### 2. **Pleuropericardial Communication**

In certain disease states, pleuropericardial adhesions or micro-fistulas may allow translocation of fibrinolytic agents from the pleural to pericardial space.

#### 3. **Systemic Absorption**

Although rare, some systemic absorption of alteplase could cause generalized bleeding tendencies, particularly in patients with coagulopathies or concomitant anticoagulation therapy.

---

### Risk Factors to Watch

Clinicians should be vigilant in patients with:

* Prior cardiac surgery or pericarditis
* Loculated effusions adjacent to the mediastinum
* Elevated INR or platelet dysfunction
* Pre-existing pericardial effusion
* High-dose or off-label alteplase use

---

### Early Recognition and Diagnosis

Early diagnosis is critical to prevent tamponade and mortality. Red flags include:

* Rapid deterioration post-IPFT
* Chest pain, shortness of breath, or syncope
* New ECG changes suggestive of pericardial involvement
* Low voltage QRS or electrical alternans
* Bedside echocardiography showing pericardial effusion

---

### Management Strategies

#### 1. **Immediate Intervention**

* Discontinue fibrinolytic therapy
* Initiate hemodynamic monitoring
* Perform urgent echocardiography
* Consider **pericardiocentesis** in unstable patients

#### 2. **Supportive Measures**

* Volume resuscitation
* Blood product replacement if hemorrhage is significant
* Surgical consultation for potential pericardial window if tamponade recurs

#### 3. **Long-Term Monitoring**

* Serial echocardiograms to assess re-accumulation
* Monitor inflammatory markers and coagulation parameters
* Consider cross-sectional imaging if pleuropericardial fistula is suspected

---

### Recommendations for Clinicians

* **Pre-IPFT Assessment**: Evaluate for pericardial disease or risk of drug leakage
* **Imaging Guidance**: Use chest CT to delineate loculation anatomy and proximity to cardiac structures
* **Dosage Control**: Avoid exceeding recommended alteplase doses
* **Post-Therapy Monitoring**: Monitor vitals, oxygenation, and perform bedside echocardiography if symptoms arise
* **Reporting**: Document and report adverse events to pharmacovigilance bodies to expand the safety database

---

### Conclusion

While IPFT with alteplase and dornase alfa remains a valuable, less-invasive alternative to surgery for complicated parapneumonic effusions, it is not without risks. **Haemorrhagic pericardial effusion**, though rare, is a serious complication requiring early detection and swift intervention.

By understanding the pathophysiology, recognizing at-risk patients, and implementing vigilant monitoring protocols, clinicians can continue to safely harness the benefits of IPFT while minimizing life-threatening complications.
