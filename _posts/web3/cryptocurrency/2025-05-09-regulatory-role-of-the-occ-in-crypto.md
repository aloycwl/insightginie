<p>The Office of the Comptroller of the Currency (OCC) has emerged as the foremost U.S. federal regulator enabling banks to engage in cryptocurrency activities—most notably through a series of interpretive letters clarifying authority for crypto custody, stablecoins, and distributed ledger networks. Since early 2025, Acting Comptroller Rodney Hood rescinded earlier restrictions and, in May, issued Interpretive Letter 1184 to explicitly permit crypto-asset custody and execution services by national banks and federal savings associations. However, mixed signals from the Federal Reserve and legal challenges underscore ongoing regulatory complexity. This article examines the OCC’s evolving crypto framework, key guidance, industry impact, and future outlook for banks navigating digital-asset services.</p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Mandate and Authority</h3>

<p>The OCC charters, regulates, and supervises all national banks and federal savings associations in the United States, ensuring a safe, sound, and competitive banking system. Historically cautious about novel financial products, the OCC has taken a proactive stance on “responsible innovation,” coordinating interagency efforts to monitor digital-asset risks.</p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Shift Under New Leadership</h3>

<p>In March 2025, Acting Comptroller Rodney Hood formally rescinded the 2022 directive under former Acting Comptroller Michael Hsu that required banks to seek prior supervisory approval for crypto activities, lowering barriers for innovation. Hood emphasized that banks must still maintain robust risk-management protocols irrespective of underlying technology.</p>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Key OCC Guidance on Cryptocurrency Activities</h2>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Crypto-Asset Custody Services</h3>

<p>Interpretive Letters 1170 and 1183, issued under Brian Brooks and Michael Hsu respectively, first recognized that national banks may provide crypto custody services in both fiduciary and non-fiduciary capacities, facilitating activities such as key safekeeping, trade execution, and reporting.</p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Interpretive Letter 1184 (May 7, 2025)</h3>

<p>The OCC’s most recent release, Interpretive Letter 1184, clarified that banks may buy and sell crypto-assets held in custody at customers’ direction and may outsource permissible activities—including custody and execution—to third parties under prudent third-party risk management practices. This letter cements banks’ authority to integrate crypto-asset services seamlessly into existing custody frameworks.</p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Stablecoin and DLT Participation</h3>

<p>Alongside custody, the OCC affirmed that national banks can engage in “certain stablecoin activities”—such as minting, redeeming, and distributing stablecoins pegged to fiat currencies—and participate in distributed ledger networks, provided they demonstrate adequate controls and compliance with BSA/AML requirements.</p>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Industry Impact and Competitive Dynamics</h2>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Banking Innovation and Market Access</h3>

<p>By reducing supervisory friction, OCC guidance has accelerated banks’ pilot programs. Major institutions are now exploring white-label custody platforms, fiat-on/off ramps, and tokenized asset settlements, aiming to capture fee income previously relegated to crypto-native firms.</p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Regulatory Tension with the Federal Reserve</h3>

<p>Despite the OCC’s permissive stance, the Federal Reserve has expressed reservations, suggesting that crypto custody may be “unsafe and unsound” absent clearer policy language. This divergence has generated market confusion, with banks awaiting a harmonized interagency framework.</p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Compliance and Risk Considerations</h3>

<p>The Anchorage Digital consent order illustrates the OCC’s supervisory rigor: despite granting custodial authority, the OCC enforced deficiencies in BSA/AML programs, signaling that operational soundness and compliance are non-negotiable.</p>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Future Outlook for Bank-Crypto Integration</h2>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Potential for Broader Crypto Services</h3>

<p>OCC letters pave the way for banks to offer lending against crypto collateral, fiduciary management of tokenized assets, and integrated payments using both stablecoins and CBDC rails as they develop.</p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Legislative and Legal Developments</h3>

<p>Congressional efforts—such as the delayed GENIUS Act—may further define bank roles in stablecoin oversight and anti-money-laundering standards, potentially reinforcing OCC guidance or imposing new guardrails.</p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Toward a Unified Regulatory Landscape</h3>

<p>Industry stakeholders advocate for a consolidated crypto-asset regulatory charter or an interagency council to align OCC, Fed, and FDIC policies, reducing legal ambiguity and fostering consistent standards across federal bank supervision.</p>
