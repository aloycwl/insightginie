Imagine you are at a dinner party, and a guest casually announces, “There is an invisible, heatless, intangible dragon living in my garage.”

You smile politely and say, “That sounds unlikely.”

The guest crosses their arms, narrows their eyes, and challenges you: “Can you prove there *isn't*?”

When you admit that you cannot run a test for an invisible, intangible creature, the guest smiles triumphantly. “Aha! Since you cannot disprove it, my dragon must exist.”

This frustrating interaction is a textbook example of the **Burden of Proof Fallacy**. It is one of the most common and manipulative tactics used in arguments, ranging from internet conspiracy theories to boardroom strategy meetings.

By shifting the responsibility of evidence onto the skeptic rather than the claimant, this fallacy creates a world where anything imaginable is considered true until proven false. To navigate the modern world of misinformation and debate, understanding the Burden of Proof is not just a philosophical exercise—it is an intellectual necessity.
