You are in a debate about politics, diet, or perhaps software engineering. You make a solid point, citing a specific example that disproves your opponent's broad generalization.

Instead of admitting they were wrong, your opponent scoffs. “Well,” they say, “that guy doesn't count. No *true* member of our group would do that.”

Suddenly, the definition of the group has changed. The goalposts have moved. You have just encountered one of the most slippery and frustrating rhetorical devices in the English language: **The No True Scotsman Fallacy**.

This fallacy is a way of protecting a universal generalization from a counterexample by arbitrarily changing the definition of the group to exclude the counterexample. It is an intellectual “trapdoor” used to escape the consequences of being wrong.

To be a sharp critical thinker and a formidable debater, you must learn to recognize this self-sealing logic and shut it down immediately.
