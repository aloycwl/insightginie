Imagine a cowboy standing in a field, holding a revolver. He aims at the side of a massive barn and unloads six shots. The bullets hit the wood in random spots, but by sheer chance, three of them land close together in the upper right corner.

The cowboy then walks up to the barn with a paintbrush. He paints a red bullseye around that tight cluster of three holes. He steps back, points to his handiwork, and declares to the town, “Look at my aim! I'm the sharpest shooter in the West.”

To an observer who arrived late, the evidence looks convincing. The holes are right in the center of the target. But to anyone paying attention, it is a fraud. He didn't hit the target; he made the target fit the shots.

This colorful anecdote is the origin of the **Texas Sharpshooter Fallacy**.

It is one of the most deceptive logical fallacies in existence because it uses real data to tell a lie. In an era obsessed with Big Data and analytics, understanding this fallacy is no longer just an academic exercise—it is a survival skill for business leaders, investors, and anyone trying to interpret the news.

What is the Texas Sharpshooter Fallacy?
---------------------------------------

The Texas Sharpshooter Fallacy is an informal fallacy that occurs when differences in data are ignored, but similarities are overemphasized. It is the act of cherry-picking a cluster of data (the bullet holes) to suit your argument, while ignoring the vast amount of data that contradicts it (the misses).

In statistical terms, it is an error of **causation**. The sharpshooter assumes that the cluster of holes exists because of his skill (cause). In reality, the cluster exists due to random variance (chance).

When we commit this fallacy, we find a pattern first and then build a hypothesis to fit it, rather than building a hypothesis and testing it against the data. We draw the target after the shots have been fired.

The Psychology: Why We Love Patterns (Apophenia)
------------------------------------------------

Why do intelligent people fall for this? The answer lies in the human brain's desperate need for order.

Humans suffer from **Apophenia**—the tendency to perceive meaningful connections between unrelated things. We see faces in clouds, animals in constellations, and trends in random stock market fluctuations.

Randomness is uncomfortable. It implies a lack of control. By drawing a “target” around a random cluster of events, we create a narrative. “This isn't just noise,” we tell ourselves. “This is a pattern.” The Texas Sharpshooter Fallacy provides a comforting, albeit false, sense of understanding.

Seeing the Sharpshooter in the Real World
-----------------------------------------

This fallacy is not limited to cowboys and barns. It pervades science, business, and even the paranormal.

### 1. The Cancer Cluster Myth

This is the most famous application of the fallacy in epidemiology.  
Imagine a country with a large population. Statistically, cancer cases will be distributed randomly. However, randomness is “clumpy.” There will naturally be neighborhoods where, by pure chance, cancer rates are slightly higher, and neighborhoods where they are slightly lower.

Residents in a “high-rate” neighborhood might look at a map, draw a circle around their town (the target), and claim, “Something here is causing cancer! It must be the power lines!”  
While environmental hazards do exist, many “cancer clusters” turn out to be statistical noise. The residents are ignoring the thousands of other towns with power lines that have average or low cancer rates (the misses).

### 2. The “Guru” Business Model

Browse the self-help or business section of a bookstore, and you will see the Texas Sharpshooter everywhere.  
A generic advice book might claim: *“All successful billionaires wake up at 4:00 AM. Therefore, waking up early causes wealth.”*

The author has drawn a target around a cluster of billionaires who are early risers. They have completely ignored:

* The billionaires who wake up at 10:00 AM (the misses).
* The broke people who wake up at 4:00 AM (the false positives).  
  By isolating a specific trait after the fact, they create a false recipe for success.

### 3. Nostradamus and Psychics

How did Michel de Nostradamus predict the rise of Hitler or the Great Fire of London in the 1500s? He didn't. He wrote thousands of vague, poetic quatrains.  
After a major historical event occurs (the bullet hole), enthusiasts scour his ancient texts looking for a verse that vaguely resembles the event. When they find one, they draw the target. “See! He knew!”  
They ignore the thousands of verses that predicted nothing or made no sense.

The Danger of Big Data
----------------------

In the 21st century, the Texas Sharpshooter has upgraded from a revolver to a machine gun.

We now have access to massive datasets. If you look at enough data variables, you are mathematically guaranteed to find correlations purely by chance. This is known as **Data Dredging** or **P-Hacking**.

For example, you could feed a supercomputer data on global weather patterns and the prices of used cars in Tokyo. The computer might find that “On Tuesdays when it rains in Brazil, the price of Toyotas in Japan drops by 0.5%.”  
Is there a causal link? No. It is a random cluster. But a “Sharpshooter” analyst might present this to a CEO as a groundbreaking market insight, leading to disastrous financial decisions.

How to Disarm the Sharpshooter
------------------------------

To avoid being fooled—or fooling yourself—you need to adopt the scientific method. Here is how to verify if a pattern is real or just a painted target.

### 1. Define the Target in Advance

This is the golden rule. You must state your hypothesis *before* you collect or look at the data.

* *Wrong:* “Let's look at the sales data and see what patterns pop up.”
* *Right:* “I believe that discounting Product A leads to higher sales of Product B. Let's analyze the data to see if this is true.”

### 2. Look for the Misses

When someone presents a startling correlation, ask to see the rest of the barn.  
If a fund manager claims, “I picked five winning stocks in a row,” ask, “How many other stocks did you pick that lost money?” If they picked 100 stocks and only highlight the five winners, they are painting a target around luck.

### 3. Replicate the Result

If you find a pattern in one dataset, test it on a completely new, fresh dataset.  
If the pattern was real (causation), it will appear again. If the pattern was a Texas Sharpshooter illusion (randomness), it will likely vanish in the new data.

Conclusion
----------

The Texas Sharpshooter Fallacy is a reminder that context is everything. Data points do not exist in a vacuum, and a cluster of “hits” means nothing without knowing the number of “misses.”

Whether you are analyzing marketing metrics, reading health news, or listening to a politician, always look for the paintbrush. Did they predict the outcome, or did they just draw a circle around what happened and call it a victory? True marksmanship is calling the shot before you pull the trigger.
