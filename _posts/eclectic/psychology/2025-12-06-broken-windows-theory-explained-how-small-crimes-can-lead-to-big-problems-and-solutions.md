Imagine walking down a street where every window is intact, the sidewalks are clean, and there's no graffiti. Now, picture another street where a single broken window remains unrepaired for days, litter is strewn about, and walls bear the marks of vandals. Which street feels safer? Which one, intuitively, seems more prone to serious crime?

This intuitive feeling is at the heart of the **Broken Windows Theory**, a powerful and often controversial concept that has profoundly influenced urban policy and policing strategies worldwide. First introduced over four decades ago, its premise is deceptively simple: visible signs of crime and disorder, like a single broken window, create an urban environment that encourages more serious crime. But how exactly does this theory work, what has been its impact, and does it still hold true today?
