The **Management Oversight and Risk Tree (MORT)** is a cornerstone of advanced risk management in industries where safety, reliability, and operational excellence are critical. When integrated with **Six Sigma methodologies**, MORT provides a systematic framework for identifying failures, evaluating barriers, and implementing corrective measures.

Understanding **MORT procedures** ensures that organizations can conduct thorough investigations, mitigate risks proactively, and optimize process performance. This article provides a detailed walkthrough of **MORT procedural steps**, highlighting practical applications and best practices.

---

### Step 1: Selecting an Episode from Barrier Analysis

MORT analysis begins with choosing a specific episode identified in a **Barrier Analysis**—a process that maps energy flows, hazards, and protective barriers in operations.

* Write the chosen episode on the MORT chart above **SA1 (“Incident”)**.
* Begin at **SB1 (“Harmful energy flow…”)**, clearly stating the energy flow and its potential to cause harm.
* Progress through the chart **top to bottom, left to right**, maintaining a systematic approach.

**Color-Coding Standards:**

* **RED:** Problem confirmed with supporting evidence.
* **GREEN:** Control measures or processes meet established standards.
* **BLUE:** Evidence insufficient or standard uncertain; further investigation required.

**Best Practices:**

* Maintain a running **list of further enquiries** to ensure no potential failure or control gap is overlooked.
* Document any **provisional Assumed Risks** during analysis for follow-up review.

---

### Step 2: Completing SB3 – Controls & Barriers LTA

After evaluating **SB3 (“Controls & Barriers Less Than Adequate”)**, the next focus is the **M-branch**, which examines management and systemic control factors.

Two approaches can be used:

1. **Ad hoc exploration** of the M-branch, following emerging insights from the SB1-SB3 analysis.
2. **Sequential exploration** using the defined nodes: **a2-MB1, a1-MB1, MA1, MA2, MB2**.

This step ensures that **organizational and management factors contributing to the incident** are systematically evaluated alongside operational controls.

---

### Step 3: Analyzing Additional Episodes

If required, select another episode from the **Barrier Analysis** and repeat the SA1 steps using a **fresh MORT chart**.

* This iterative approach allows multiple risk scenarios to be examined independently.
* Ensures that **all critical episodes in the process** are evaluated for potential oversights and deficiencies.

---

### Step 4: Moving to SA2 – Amelioration

Once **all SA1 analyses** are complete:

* Mark which episodes from the Barrier Analysis have undergone MORT analysis.
* Move to **SA2 (“Amelioration”)**, focusing on corrective and mitigating measures after incidents.
* Explore the **M-branch** in light of SA2 findings, assessing whether management controls adequately support risk mitigation.

This phase shifts attention from **incident causation** to **post-event management and process improvement**.

---

### Step 5: Reviewing Provisional Assumed Risks

Provisional Assumed Risks noted in previous steps must be carefully reviewed:

* Assess if they are **Less Than Adequate (LTA)** using nodes such as **a1-MB1**.
* Evaluate whether these assumed risks require additional controls, procedural changes, or further investigation.

This step ensures that **all known and potential risks are addressed** systematically before finalizing recommendations.

---

### Step 6: Reviewing MB2

Next, review **MB2 (“Management Branch 2”)** in light of the analysis conducted so far:

* Confirm that **management and oversight functions** are properly evaluated.
* Identify gaps in policies, supervision, training, or resources that could compromise safety or operational efficiency.

This step integrates **SA1, SA2, and M-branch insights** into a coherent assessment of both operational and managerial factors.

---

### Step 7: Overview of M-Branch Issues

The final procedural step is a comprehensive review of all **M-branch issues**:

* Consider the **overall systemic risk picture**, integrating findings from specific and amelioration branches.
* Document the results of the analysis, including **causal factors, risk controls, and recommended improvements**.
* Ensure that lessons learned feed into **future Barrier Analyses and Six Sigma process improvements**.

This holistic review solidifies the value of MORT as a **structured tool for risk management** and ensures that all contributing factors are captured.

---

### Practical Tips for MORT Procedure Implementation

1. **Use Evidence-Based Coding:** Only assign **RED, GREEN, or BLUE** based on verifiable data. Avoid assumptions that could compromise the reliability of the analysis.
2. **Document Assumed Risks:** Keep a running table of **provisional and confirmed assumed risks** to ensure no detail is overlooked.
3. **Iterate When Needed:** Complex operations may require multiple MORT charts and repeated analyses of episodes to cover all risks comprehensively.
4. **Integrate with Six Sigma:** Apply findings to **DMAIC or DFSS projects**, ensuring that risk insights lead to measurable process improvements.
5. **Engage Trained Analysts:** MORT procedures are **professional tools**, requiring experience and judgment to apply correctly.

---

### Conclusion

MORT procedures provide a **step-by-step, systematic method** for identifying operational failures, management oversights, and assumed risks. By combining **Barrier Analysis, SA1 and SA2 evaluations, and thorough M-branch exploration**, organizations can ensure comprehensive **risk identification, mitigation, and continuous improvement**.

When integrated with **Six Sigma initiatives**, MORT not only enhances **process safety and reliability** but also supports **data-driven decision-making, root cause analysis, and operational excellence**.

Mastering MORT procedures equips organizations with a **powerful toolset to proactively manage risks, prevent losses, and sustain high standards of safety and performance**.
